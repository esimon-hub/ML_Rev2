A leap of faith: Reforming Argentina power sector
May DD, 2026
From Crisis to Cautious Optimism
Argentina is undergoing a major transformation that extends beyond the energy sector. President Javier Milei, who assumed office in late 2023, has initiated a series of bold reforms aimed at revitalizing the economy and rebuilding market confidence. As a result, the country is once again capturing the attention of global investors. In the power sector, a wave of regulatory reforms is also reshaping the market to stimulate private investment and competitiveness. 
However, Argentina’s history has been marked by economic volatility and policy instability, creating a complex landscape that breeds skepticism among investors. The power market is characterized by frequent policy shifts, high levels of government intervention, and a fragmented legal environment that undermines investors’ predictability. [FT: See Argentina power market profile].  
Amidst these challenges, power demand growth has been largely driven by the residential sector, supported by frozen tariffs. In contrast, industrial demand remains stagnant at levels seen in 2005-06. Capacity additions have depended on government-led programs [FT: FONIVEMEM, Fund for Necessary Investment to Boost Electricity Supply, Energy Plus and RenovAr], yet many projects have faced delays or cancellations due to financial constraints. Besides, decades of underinvestment in transmission have also limited the development of renewables potential and the main demand center, Buenos Aires region, has faced several blackouts over the past years.  
In this context, the current reform agenda seeks to address longstanding issues and inefficiencies of the power market, while fostering a more transparent and stable framework for investors. A series of regulatory changes were enacted recently to address issues across the entire power sector value chain. Notably, Resolution 21/2025 and Resolution 400/25 mark the most significant reform in over a decade and could pave the way for a gradual transition to a liberalized market.
 

The gradual path to market opening
The need for a power sector reform to improve market conditions was clear. But rather than embrace a sudden break with the past, the government chose a more measured and gradual transition to ensure operational stability. Legacy structures will continue to partially exist, while important changes were made to address the most pressing distortions in the power sector. 
Among these distortions was the role of the power system operator, CAMMESA [FT: Compañía Administradora del Mercado Mayorista Eléctrico]. Over the last decades, CAMMESA’s responsibilities have expanded, making it the financial and operational heart of Argentina's power system. The entity has become the central power purchasing agent and responsible for managing fuel supply of thermal plants. This centralization has led to a market in which the state essentially absorbed all financial risks. 
Bilateral contracts on the rise 
The reinstatement of the term market (bilateral market) marks the revival of private contracting, thus reducing the state’s role as the central counterparty. Over the recent past, bilateral contracts were only permitted through the MATER framework [FT: Mercado a Término de Energía Renovable] focused on renewable energy sources and leaving conventional power practically restricted to state control [FT: with a few legacy exceptions, such as contracts from Energy Plus program (2006)]. Consequently, most large users remained bound to CAMMESA’s centralized procurement, leaving them exposed to administered prices and the system’s fiscal and operational volatility. 
With the reform, all large users are not only permitted but required to procure their energy directly in the wholesale market, either through the term market or spot purchases. Besides, new [FT: commercial operation date after January 1st, 2025] conventional power plants, and existing thermal plants that manage their own fuel are also allowed to sign bilateral contracts. It is the first time large users will be freely negotiating their energy since the 2001 crisis.
While large consumers may face greater exposure to the spot market volatility in the very short-term, this change is expected to accelerate the development of the bilateral term market in the medium term. We estimate that since the reform entered into force, at least 4 GW of projects have already signed contracts, mostly thermal plants. About 14 GW of capacity are currently operating in the spot market only and should eventually also sign PPAs. 
Users will increasingly seek competitive and flexible contract terms to hedge against price fluctuations and lower their electricity bills. Additionally, removing CAMMESA as an intermediary mitigates risk associated with historical payment delays to generators, fostering a more stable investment environment.
But restoring private contracting in the power sector also required tackling the other side of the generation equation: the centralized management of fuel supply.

Generators take charge of fuel management
Since 2013 [FT: Resolution SE 95/2013], fuel supply for power generation in Argentina was centrally managed by CAMMESA, which procured and delivered fuels to generators [FT: Players were able to procure their own fuel for a short period during Mauricio Macri’s government (SE 70/2018), but the measure was revoked a year later (SE 12/2019)]. Domestic natural gas purchases were mostly done through PlanGas  program, and LNG imports and liquid fuels were acquired through international tenders held either by Enarsa or directly by CAMMESA. Under this model, fuel risks were borne by the state, and generators had no financial incentive to improve plant efficiency. The focus was on power plant availability, and with thin profit margins, generators limited their investments. 
To unwind this centralized model, the government has set a course toward a fully open fuel market by 2029. Since March-2025 [FT: Resolution 21/2025], thermal generators can independently procure their own fuel. While existing PlanGas contracts will remain in effect until expiration [FT: last contract expires in Dec-2028 ], the government has created a provisional model that allows reduction of PlanGas volumes and transfer of contracts to private players [FT: Resolution 501/2025, Resolution 606/2025]. At the same time, the wholesale market reform created incentives for generators to shift to self-managed supply since Nov-2025. In the first quarter of 2026, 30% of the gas consumption in the power sector was from self-managed plants, a jump from 5% in 2025. 
The new scheme is a major step toward a liberalized market, shifting procurement responsibility to generators and fostering competition on fuel prices, plant efficiency and fuel logistics . But making this shift to self-managed fuel supply viable also required fixing the underlying price signals in the spot market.
Reestablishment of marginal cost-based payments
Since 2003, spot prices were capped to shield consumers from soaring inflation. However, decoupling spot payments from real marginal costs have created significant market distortions. Generators’ revenue was frequently below actual production costs, and the state often absorbed this gap with subsidies. This intervention culminated in 2013 [FT: Resolution 95/2013], when an administrative remuneration scheme was created (Energía de Base), which essentially dismantled the spot market and left generators financially dependent on the state. 
The new framework aims to restore spot market payments under a marginal cost scheme. The capped spot price has been abolished and spot payments for new [FT: commercial operation date after January 1st, 2025] projects and thermals with own fuel management have been set as the system’s marginal cost since Nov-2025. Payments for existing projects, however, will still be anchored to a regulated scheme, yet will gradually incorporate marginal costs signals [FT: For 2026, 15% of the marginal cost was incorporated in their remuneration and is expected to reach 35% by 2028. A full implementation timeline has not yet been scheduled]. Besides, payments are now established in dollar terms. 
This phased approach aims to ensure a smoother transition, improve investment conditions, and encourage generators to take control of their own fuel supply. Still, normalizing remuneration at the generation level could not, on its own, restore the sector’s financial equilibrium. The other part of the problem lies in the fact that wholesale costs were disconnected from end-user tariffs. 
Reducing the impact on priority sectors
Years of artificially depressed seasonal prices  and frozen distribution margins severed the link between the cost of electricity and the price consumers paid. That disconnect broke the payment chain, leaving distribution companies deeply indebted to CAMMESA. While the reform ultimately aims to reconnect retail tariffs with market realities, exposing households and small businesses to an immediate price shock would have carried a high political cost.
To mitigate this impact, the reform established a protected demand tier, explicitly defining residential and commercial users as priority consumers. Under this structure, priority users will be supplied primarily through legacy contracts and centralized state procurement [FT: Includes CAMMESA supply contracts, hydro plants under national concession, binational power plants (Yacyretá/Salto Grande), nuclear plants managed by NASA and centralized energy imports]. By ring-fencing this segment   of the market, the reform limits these users’ direct exposure to spot market volatility and increased prices. 
That protection, however, is transitional. Distribution companies are now required to have at least 75% of their demand covered by contracts. As legacy contracts expire, distributors will need to procure energy in the term market, gradually reducing CAMMESA’s intermediate role.
New rules, old concerns
Taken together, these reforms amount to a gradual but meaningful reconfiguration of Argentina’s power sector. The government is not dismantling the centralized model overnight. Instead, it is opening the market in stages: first by reviving bilateral contracting for large users, then by shifting fuel procurement back to generators, reintroducing marginal cost-based price signals, and only gradually exposing the retail segment to market-based procurement. 

There are already early signs of recovery in investor sentiment. The response to recent initiatives, including the energy storage auction [FT1] and the privatization of hydro and transmission assets [FT2], suggests that market participants are willing to re-engage where regulatory signals are improving. In parallel, RIGI-approved investments [FT3] of roughly $ 30 billion — particularly in oil, gas, and mining — could support a new cycle of power demand growth.
Yet the old concerns remain. Reducing the annual inflation rate, continuity in fiscal prudence, sustaining economic growth and reducing the country's solvency risk remains a challenge. The central question is not whether the reforms points in the right direction, but whether these market signals can be sustained through the country’s recurrent cycles of economic volatility and policy reversal. While opportunities in the power sector are becoming more tangible, investor confidence still involves a leap of faith.
